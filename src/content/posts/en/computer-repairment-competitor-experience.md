# Preface

It has already been a year since the competition in November 2022, and I still hadn’t properly paid off this “competitor write-up debt”.... Even bubu and our skating sister kept reminding me to write it XD. Looks like it’s finally time to settle the debt....

This post probably won’t be too long (update: no, it’s very long), but you’ll get to see the intense journey of a student from a rural area with almost no resources, who relied on stubbornness and perseverance to carve out a path at the national competition and ultimately win the championship. I believe everyone reading this will be satisfied with the story!

# Background

:::note
Let me introduce myself first. I’m an Information Technology student from Ruifang, New Taipei. After entering vocational high school, I only set one goal for myself: get into the top vocational university, NTUST, through the recommendation program. So during my first and second year, I mostly focused on keeping my grades high, and since I was also the president of the Breaking dance club, that was basically my whole life.
:::

In the second semester of my second year, the department director told me, “I hope you can represent the school in the Industrial Technology Competition.” Of course I wasn’t going to miss an opportunity that special XD. I had three categories to choose from: Computer Software Design, Robotics, and Computer Repair. Even though I ended up competing in Computer Repair, my original preference order was actually Software Design > Robotics > Computer Repair. It was only because the director and Wenxian, my advisor, strongly recommended it XD that I ended up going into Computer Repair.

But I want to clarify something first: the Computer Repair category is not about repairing computers! Our job is server administration, network management, and Arduino communication programming. We focus on Windows and Linux server roles and deployment, which is much closer to a school or enterprise network administrator position.

Anyway, that’s how I entered the training room~. The decision was made in February, but I only gradually started taking over the training room in June, and it wasn’t until July that intensive training really began.

# Training

## Walking Straight Into a Trap?

Back then I entered the training room full of excitement, but just a few days later my mindset exploded....

First of all, **almost no useful materials had been left behind**. What had been passed down from previous years was practically yellowed with age, so it was basically ancient. Some system syntax and architecture had already changed completely, and some of it had even been abandoned entirely. I simply could not use any of it. In fact, it would only slow down my practice.

Then there was the senior student, who was unbelievably terrible.... His legendary deeds included: bringing code written by Wenxian into the competition venue and then not understanding any of it himself; placing second to last in the competition, with the last-place person only ranking below him because they got COVID and didn’t even compete; and worst of all, he secretly took tools out of my toolkit without my permission, and I caught him on the spot ==. He also did not guide me at all in the early stages of practice. Well, obviously, because he was that useless. So I had to face everything alone.

Finally, there was my advisor.... Wenxian had his own classes to teach and also had to manage his evening study homeroom, so he simply didn’t have time to focus on the competitor side of things. On top of that, the school’s competition results in previous years had only been average, so he wasn’t very familiar with the actual competition content either. Yes, I also thought that was kind of wild. He could only guide me through some very basic knowledge, like Class C and Class B certificate question banks. Everything else was basically left to chance.... Sometimes I was even the one answering his questions.

OK, let’s summarize: a terrible senior, no data, and an advisor who didn’t really understand the competition content. No wonder the previous seniors hadn’t done particularly well. Faced with this kind of “hell-mode opening,” most people would probably just give up, stop caring, or even drop out halfway through.

At the time, I told the department director about the situation. He only said, “It’s okay. Just take care of your schoolwork first. Academics come first. At least you’ll still have school.” That was the situation I faced when I took over in June. Even the director felt there was probably not much hope. So how was a competitor supposed to face a challenge like this?

But I do want to clarify that the director was actually a very responsible teacher. Even though he said that, he also told me that if I lacked any resources, I could let him know, and if I needed materials for practice, he would take care of purchasing them.

Hearing that, I realized I was in an environment with extremely scarce resources and heavy peer competition. I couldn’t enjoy the kind of school resources that traditional powerhouse schools had. Maybe that is exactly why schools gradually decline: the strong schools get stronger, and the weak schools get weaker.

But since I’m writing this article, there must have been a turning point. So let’s keep going.

## Refusing to Lose

Facing that kind of situation was truly painful, even hopeless at times, but I still didn’t want to face the version of myself that gave up or acted weak. That unwillingness to lose was ignited right then and there. “You think I can’t do it? Then I’ll prove it to you. I can. I’m not just some grain of sand buried in a crowd.”

So I started planning my practice schedule. From July until the competition in mid-November, I had four months of training time. In between, there were also four training camps and mock competitions organized by New Taipei City. That meant I needed to reach a certain level by mid-August, and then spend the remaining time gradually filling in the details.

So an initial plan came together: first train the broad structure, then train the details, and gradually fill out every branch of skill, almost like leveling up abilities in a game.

## The Willingness to Ask

Since I didn’t have many resources, I could only move forward one step at a time, with Google trying to rescue my competitor life. But there is still one person I want to thank: the previous year’s champion. We knew each other because of dance, and by coincidence, he had also once been a competitor in this same category. Whenever I really got stuck and couldn’t figure something out, I shamelessly went to ask him for help. He would share some of his experience and technical keywords, like a small guiding light pointing the way. Even though he only gave me a few brief hints, they helped me tremendously.

We don’t dance together anymore now, but if we ever meet again in the future, senior, I’ll treat you to a meal.

## A Competitor Is the Definition of Contradiction

“Competitor” is a contradictory word. Normally, I pursue “efficiency.” I don’t want to spend too much time on trivial things. But the process of competition training completely broke that rule.

We constantly had to focus on tiny details and improve speed through repetitive practice. For example, crimping network cables is an extremely simple task, but to increase my speed, I made well over a hundred of them. I even developed calluses on my hands. At first, my hands were soft and would turn red after just a few cables, but later I could calmly and smoothly finish four or five without blinking.

I once calculated that if it took me 12 minutes to make one cable, then six cables would take 72 minutes. I absolutely could not spend more than an hour on something as stupid as “making network cables.” So I practiced until it became muscle memory. In the end, I got it down to around 5 minutes each. Six cables then took about 30 minutes, cutting the time by more than half. In a competition that values perfection and speed, every minute and second can be fatal.

We were also particular about tool placement. The computer in the center, power strip in the back, soldering iron on the right, tools on the left. The purpose was to make everything foolproof and keep the training environment as close as possible to the real competition site, reducing the chance of mistakes and making the process more familiar.

I was also strict about food. For the two weeks before the competition, I only ate 7-Eleven salads and made sure my stomach stayed completely normal, all to eliminate the possibility of losing because of food on competition day. If anything went wrong, the trophy could slip very far away. That kind of detail mattered to me too.

## Physically and Mentally Exhausted

One and a half months before the competition, the department officially pulled me out of regular classes, as they do for all competitors, and moved me fully into the training room so I could focus entirely on the competition.

Daily schedule:
| Time | Activity |
| ---- | --------- |
| 8:00 ~ 12:00 | Practice |
| 12:00~12:40 | Lunch |
| 13:00-17:00 | Practice |
| 17:00-17:40 | Dinner |
| 17:40-21:20 | Practice |
| 21:30 | Head home |

That lasted for about a month and a half. Most of the practice was done alone. There was another competitor in the room, but we were from different categories, so at most we only exchanged a sentence or two when we were tired. That was it. So during those dull training days, I was basically accompanied only by the computer, the soldering board, and, well, Google.

Still, I really liked lunch and dinner time, because I would go eat with two students from the Electronics department. On the way to meals, we often talked about what we had practiced that day. Even though we were all from different categories and each had our own stories, in those moments we could understand each other’s feelings and vent the loneliness and pressure that had built up inside. Honestly, that was probably the happiest part of the whole period, second only to winning the award itself.

At the end of each day of practice, I would take the second-to-last Route 788 bus from Ruifang to Keelung. The night was dim, my heart was tired, my body was tired, and I often felt exhausted while sitting on the bus. At times like that, I would imagine myself standing on the award stage, draped in the school flag, holding the trophy, the center of attention. The moment I thought about that, the exhaustion suddenly felt smaller. Maybe it was worth continuing. If the effort now could be exchanged for future achievement, then that was a pretty good deal.

But I do need to complain: the 788 bus often skipped runs, which meant I sometimes couldn’t catch any bus at all. Sometimes I would wait from 9:20 all the way until the final bus at 10:30, cursing inwardly the whole time about what a broken rural bus system this was. And back then I wasn’t even 18 yet, so I couldn’t ride a scooter home. I often just waited and waited by the road. To kill time, I made a decision for myself: since I’m already here, why not go ahead and prepare for the GEPT Intermediate-High exam?

So I started memorizing high school level 5 and 6 vocabulary. That became my main weapon against the boredom of waiting for the bus, and during that period I ended up preparing not only for the competition, but also for the GEPT on the side, which I eventually passed.

## The Security Guards

Whenever I think about my competitor years, I always think of the school’s security guards, because I was often the last person to leave school. I chatted with them a lot. They worked very hard too. Late at night they had to go lock up the buildings, and if an office window or door wasn’t closed, they had to run back and check it again.

One time, because the bus had skipped its run, there was no bus for me to take at all. At that moment, one of the guards happened to be going home. He rode out on his little scooter, stopped in front of the bus stop, and said, “Come on, I’ll take you home!”

That’s how one of my unforgettable memories was written. He gave me his extra half-helmet, and we rode all the way to the bus stop near my house. I still remember it was around 10:40 that night. I was truly grateful that he gave me a ride home. Otherwise I would have had to call a taxi... and a poor student like me really couldn’t bear spending that money.

Sadly, not long after that, he retired because of illness, and I never saw him again. But he still left a mark on my heart.

Throughout my competitor journey, I got to know three security guards in total. Sometimes when the bus couldn’t be trusted, ~I had no choice but to throw myself into the embrace of the security guards~. I would stay with them while they locked up the campus until eleven, and then they would take me home. Along the way, we would chat, and I could tell they were a bit lonely too, just like me, so once they started talking, they could go on forever. They would joke about which office always forgot to close the door, which teacher was slacking off, who was the nicest teacher, and so on. It added a little color to the otherwise dull “road of locking up the school.”

During winter break, I even went back to chat with them again. They happily shared what had happened during that time, and it felt as if we had returned to that year again. It made me cherish those memories even more, even if they were technically only side-story material.

## Mock Competitions

Actually, I think my mock competitions went pretty smoothly. I was almost always at the top. At most there was one time I got second place because I had COVID, but other than that, I took first place in every New Taipei City mock competition. I think by the later stage it really just came down to who had faster reactions and more flexible improvisation, because as time passed, everyone gradually filled in the required knowledge. What remained was familiarity and adaptability.

I never became proud or complacent just because I kept placing first in the mock competitions. I still believed the people outside were stronger, and if I underestimated them, I could take a serious hit in the real competition.

![](https://imgur.com/kmyLafg.jpg)

Maybe it was because I came from a rural area and had also seen more of the outside world, but I understood that you can’t only focus your eyes on what is right in front of you. Often, the real challenge lies beyond what you can currently see. That mindset also applied to being a competitor.

So right up until the competition, I kept my head down and continued fixing my weaknesses. I listed out my weak points and corrected them one by one, hoping to make myself a “flawless” competitor.

Back then, if there was a skill I needed, I would squat down and ask another competitor about it. Even if they weren’t as strong overall as I was, so what? I only knew one thing: they knew something I didn’t. So I asked. If it could help me improve, why shouldn’t I shamelessly go over and ask?

![](https://imgur.com/q49X69f.jpg)

That was my attitude.

As for how I later became the instructor for the next year, that is [another story](https://blog.vicwen.app/posts/newtaipeicity-computer-repairment-camp-lecturer-records/)....

# The Industrial Technology Competition

## The Day Before

The moment that had to come finally arrived. The two-day Industrial Technology Competition opened at Nangang Vocational High School. The day before the competition, we carried all of our tools and computers to the venue to set everything up and familiarize ourselves with the site. As always, there were a lot of people. In one of the most crowded categories in the entire competition, 61 competitors gathered there to fight for ranking over the next two days.

On the bus, I chatted casually with Wenxian, and he asked me, “What place do you want?” I answered immediately: “Obviously first place. Today we’re bringing back a Gold Medal first place trophy for you. You’ve probably never seen one before.”

We both laughed happily, just trying to dilute the tension before the competition.

## Day One

On the first day, the morning station covered server deployment and network management, followed by the written test in the afternoon.

As soon as I walked into the venue, I could feel the killing intent in the air. Everyone was holding their breath and concentrating. I also used that time to quickly confirm my materials and tools while waiting for the competition to begin. Wenxian cheered me on from the side, reminding me to be careful, not overlook any details, and to ask questions during the problem briefing if anything was unclear.

When the time came, all advisors were asked to leave the venue and the space was cleared. We competitors were taken into a meeting room where the rules and competition content were explained. The moment I saw the test sheet, I estimated that I would either get only one item wrong or even get a perfect score. Besides the required tasks, there were two items that looked like real differentiators: an NTP Server and Windows Sandbox.

I had encountered the first one before. It had appeared in the 2016 competition questions, and by coincidence, that was exactly the oldest set of questions I had practiced. Anything earlier than that used Linux as the server, which no longer matched the current technical context. So I had basically hit the jackpot....

The second one, Sandbox, I had never touched before. But I roughly guessed where it might be installed, so I planned to rely on improvisation in the moment.

After the explanation ended, we all returned to our seats and the countdown began. Three and a half hours. The large red digital clock hung right above my upper right side, where I could see time slipping away second by second. I think the most painful part early on was having to make six network cables.... It was unbearable. There were just too many. Usually there had only been four.

As I kept going, I eventually finished everything I was supposed to do, and the last challenge left was figuring out Sandbox. After some effort, I completed it! And there was still an hour and a half left. After checking everything several times, I raised my hand and waited for scoring.

I thought I had everything correct, but I had missed one detail: the usernames.... Let me explain. We had to create three types of users. Two of the user types had IDs `12345`, but the one in the middle unexpectedly had to be `0102030405`. It was unbelievably annoying. Because of that alone, I lost ten points, plus another ten points in time score, twenty points total. I never expected that I’d get the hard parts right and then lose points over such a weird trap question. My mood was terrible. I had thrown away 20 points because of one small oversight.

After lunch came the written test. I didn’t think it was very hard, and I estimated I got around 90.

On the way back, I told Wenxian what had happened. He said I had already done very well and told me to go back and prepare properly for the next day’s tasks so I could turn things around. That helped my mood a little.

## Day Two

The second day covered the second station: Arduino, VB.NET programming, and soldering the circuit board, followed by the closing and partial score announcements in the afternoon.

I decided not to care about what had happened the day before and to focus only on the present. I was very confident in the second station, because I had put a lot of extra work into programming, so I estimated that I would get a perfect score there.

Once the competition started, I honestly felt the questions were written pretty vaguely, but all we could do was try our best to guess what the judges actually wanted XD. The total time was three and a half hours. I spent about forty to fifty minutes soldering and another forty minutes writing code.

What was a bit unfortunate was that I didn’t correctly guess the intended direction for the code section, so I basically had to rewrite almost everything. If I had guessed correctly from the start, I think I could have compressed my time even more. In the end, including checking, I finished the entire second station in one hour and forty minutes, with two full hours still left before the competition ended. So I gathered up my courage and raised my hand to request scoring XD.

During scoring, there were a few places where what the judge intended was different from what I had understood. But during scoring, we had the opportunity to explain our thinking and “persuade” the judges, so in two sections I actually argued with them XD. I had implemented things exactly according to the wording of the problem statement, so if we were being precise, you couldn’t really say I was wrong. Luckily, because I explained myself, I managed to protect my score.

After that nerve-racking scoring process, I headed to the conference hall in a much better mood and waited for the competition to end~

One interesting thing was that I was the first person to enter the conference hall, which makes sense. The second person came in about half an hour later, and the third another half hour after that. Only then did the others gradually arrive. So I was honestly pretty bored in there, and we didn’t even have our phones QQ.

At the time, I roughly estimated I would still finish in the top five, but exactly where, I truly couldn’t say. Because of the first-station mistake, I thought I might not get first. But I also felt that if I didn’t get first, I would be letting myself down. So while waiting, my heart was under a lot of pressure.

## Returning from Battle

Once everyone was ready, the advisors also gradually entered. Each competitor sat in the conference hall with their advisor, waiting for the judging panel to announce the results. Before the real announcement, they gave the usual remarks: thank you all for your hard work, being a competitor is just one chapter in life, keep moving forward, and so on.

Then they started announcing the scores. One sentence instantly made me feel relieved: “The highest score for Station One this time was 80, so Station Two will be scaled up by 20 points across the board, with a maximum of 100.” The moment I heard that, I almost cried. Didn’t that mean I had the top score in Station One! And since I had gotten everything correct in Station Two, I was almost certain at that point that I was first place.

When we walked out of the conference hall and started packing our things to head back to Ruifang, I suddenly noticed the written-test answers posted outside. I calculated my score and got 92, which was about what I had expected. So I happily headed back to school with Wenxian~ and we even had a celebration dinner that night.

But I still kept quiet about it for the moment XD. Better to wait until the official results were announced the next day~.

# Conclusion

I’m glad that after working hard for months, I got to welcome the best possible ending.

I think competition is 90% effort and 10% luck, so I consider myself a very lucky person. Effort alone could only push my ranking forward, but it was that last 10% of luck that carried me all the way to first place.
