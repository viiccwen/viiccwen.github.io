# Preface

Ever since I won first place in the Computer Repair category last year, I originally thought I would finally take off my contestant jersey and focus on college. But at the beginning of this August, the instructor who had trained our cohort suddenly reached out to me. Because he was too busy to serve as a lecturer for New Taipei City's 2023 training camp, he hoped I could take the role instead. And so, by a twist of fate, I found myself stepping back into the world of contestants once again, except this time my perspective had shifted from below the stage to on top of it.

# The Process

Once I took on the lecturer role, all the feelings of being a contestant came rushing back. I still had to stay tense, predict possible competition questions, and even write questions, except this time they were for the younger students. During the training period, I started preparing slides, releasing component information, announcing circuits, explaining competition notes, and so on. There were all kinds of scattered details that had to be passed on to them. At the same time, I realized again just how hard contestants have it, and it brought back memories of that brutal sleep-deprived period.... From the start of preparation to finishing the lecture materials, it took a full week. I revised them again and again, cutting away the unnecessary, because I did not want the younger students to drown in too much documentation, but I also did not want them to miss anything important. I also gave them, free of charge, the program architecture and coding style I used in my own competition back then. Clear naming and a clean, organized coding area can greatly reduce the time spent writing and debugging programs. In the past, because I had suffered enough under VB6, I angrily ported everything over to VB .NET, and this time that decision finally paid off, allowing everyone to switch to writing in .NET. Much more comfortable....

Some people also asked me, "Aren't you worried about giving everything away to others?" For me, first of all, programming does not take up that large a portion in this category, so I do not think it is a big deal. Second, there is that feeling you have as a teacher: you want your students to learn as much as possible. More importantly, competitions ultimately depend on the individual. People who are willing to work hard and fight for it will still shine even in the darkest night. Even without my help, I can say with confidence that their final ranking would not be affected too much. People will always find a way forward. So even if I prepared everything for a student and even wrote the code for them, if they were unwilling to put in the effort, they would absolutely end up as cannon fodder, just like one of my seniors.

# Difficulties

In the first class, because it was my first time teaching this kind of course, I did not manage the time or pace very well, and some people even asked questions I had not anticipated. I honestly felt pretty sorry toward the younger students for that. So in the classes that followed, I gradually adjusted the way I spoke, fine-tuned the rhythm of the course, and evaluated the students' condition so I could deepen the content when appropriate. Through this, I also learned a lot of tricks and experience about "teaching." Even though I have always considered myself pretty good at "teaching others," there is still a lot of room for improvement.

Coming up with mock competition questions was also mentally exhausting. I revised every set more than ten times from beginning to end. The documentation was especially the hardest part to edit. Change one tiny detail, and every related section has to be updated. Then after that, I still had to check everything multiple times before I dared to close my laptop and rest. After each round, I also had to adjust the next mock exam according to everyone's level. I can only say this definitely is not easy work QQ.

# Expectations

There were eight students, and I treated every one of them like my own juniors. Some of them even messaged me at one or two in the morning, and I still tried my best to answer them. My mindset is: "I am not afraid of you asking questions, I am only afraid of you not asking." The more you ask, the more likely you are to dig out little tricks I forgot to mention. The more you practice, and the broader you practice, the more likely it is that you will happen to predict part of the competition content, and that means you gain an advantage. Miss one problem, and the ranking gap between you and others opens right up. During practice, seeing everyone's bright, focused eyes and their willingness to put in effort, practice, and solve problems honestly made me very happy. Even though I was not the one competing, as your teacher (an extra one), I still hoped every one of you would earn great results, and maybe even come become my juniors someday XD.

![](https://imgur.com/JKMdFwM.jpg)

# Final Farewell, Waiting for Your Good News

Before the final mock competition, I seriously told everyone the key things to pay attention to in the real competition, how to adjust their mindset, and tricks for scoring points quickly. I only hoped all of you could achieve excellent results. I also left everyone with one sentence: "When you step up there, go in wanting to crush the entire field. You are the strongest." To be honest, that was also exactly what I was thinking back when I walked into the competition venue myself: I am the best, and I am here to dominate everyone. Maybe it sounds childish, but it was the most effective kind of motivation. In the end, I watched each student leave the classroom one by one, ready to make their final push and welcome the day they would step onto the stage.

From our first training session to the moment everyone left after the final mock competition, I saw all of your changes, the effort you put in, and that reckless determination, presence, and attitude to keep moving forward. I am really looking forward to hearing your good news.

# Closing

The 2023 school year National Industrial Skills Competition for senior high school students officially came to an end on November 23 this year. Among the eight younger students I trained in the Computer Repair category, every single one of them placed among the award winners. There were eight Golden Hand awards in this category in total, and New Taipei City took half of them, including the championship. At the time, I was watching the livestream in front of my computer. When I saw that five of the top ten were ours, and that we had even taken first place, I was just as happy as all of you. I was also glad that the many months you spent grinding away in the contestant room finally paid off. That sense of honor from standing on the award stage is the most precious thing of all.
