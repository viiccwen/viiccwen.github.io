> This article is an excerpt from [my SITCON 2025 submission](https://sitcon.org/2025/agenda/62f54e/)

In student life, we all get the chance to join clubs, student organizations, or even build our own communities. But the shift from "managing" to "leading" does not happen overnight. In this article, I will share my experience with community leadership: how to get started, how to lead, and how to truly create impact.

# Why choose leadership?
If you have ever served as a club officer or joined a large event, you may have realized that a "leader" and a "manager" are not the same role. A manager keeps things running smoothly, while a leader is the person who inspires the team and drives change.

In my experience, being a community leader not only gave me a lot of hands-on practice, but also taught me how to work with different people, solve problems, and even lead a team through adversity.


# How do you start becoming a community leader?
## 1. Campus clubs
If you are still in school, clubs are a great starting point. My own path looked like this:

1. Join a club - find one you are interested in and actively take part in its activities.
2. Be active in the club - do more than just "show up"; speak up and contribute your ideas.
3. Let others know you want to become an officer - once the current officers know you are interested, opportunities will come naturally.
4. Become an officer - start with small tasks, build experience step by step, and eventually become a core member of the club.

## 2. Student community conferences
Beyond campus clubs, student community conferences are also a great opportunity, such as COSCUP, DevFest, SITCON, and HITCON. Many people think events like these are just for listening to talks, but in reality:

> 20% listening to talks, 80% making friends!

They are a chance to build connections and meet people who share your interests.

So when you attend these events, remember to talk to people actively instead of just sitting in the audience. For your first time, you can even ask friends nearby whether they are going too, so you can go together and feel less nervous 🏃


# From "managing" to "leading"
Many club officers focus on the "management" side of things, such as:
* Making sure events run smoothly
* Handling internal administrative work
* Managing community members

Of course, these are important. But to become a true leader, you need to go one step further:

* Build a vision - let the team know what we are trying to achieve, and help everyone feel connected to that goal.
* Motivate the team - everyone goes through low points; keeping the team energized is one of a leader's key jobs.
* Expand influence - do not just manage internal affairs; build external partnerships so the community can grow.


# Why are leaders important?
Why is the role of a "leader" so important? I think the key is:

> Internal management + external growth = success!

If you only focus on internal management, the team may become conservative and stop growing.

If you only focus on external development, the internal side can become loose and lack cohesion. A true leader needs to balance both.


# Expand your network and create more opportunities
Through my experience as a community leader, I found that **relationships** are an extremely important resource. Through community events and meetups, I not only met many excellent people, but also gained a lot of valuable opportunities.

## How can you build your network effectively?
1. Take the initiative - do not be shy; be brave and meet new people.
2. Talk sincerely - do not do it only for personal gain; genuinely want to get to know the other person.
3. Stay in touch - once you meet someone, keep the connection alive so future collaboration becomes possible.

## What resources can help us grow?
In the process of becoming a community leader, I found many resources that can help us improve leadership skills, broaden our horizons, and open up more possibilities. Here are some I have personally experienced and would recommend if you want to keep growing:

### 1. Campus clubs: the best starting point

Clubs are the most direct and easiest growth platform to join. Whether you serve as an officer or participate actively, you can accumulate a lot of valuable experience. For example:
* Student councils and self-governance groups: learn organizational management, administrative operations, and communication skills.
* Technical clubs (such as GDG on Campus and AI Club): strengthen professional skills and connect with industry.
* Interest-based clubs (such as dance or music clubs): build teamwork and creative thinking.

> Tip: Start by attending activities, speaking up, and offering suggestions. Then work toward becoming an officer.


### 2. Cross-school events: broaden your view and build connections
Besides campus clubs, cross-school events let you meet more like-minded people and talk to people from different backgrounds. Examples include:
* Hackathons: team up with students from other schools to solve problems and improve your technical and collaboration skills.
* Community conferences (such as DevFest, PyCon, COSCUP): attend talks and connect with people from the industry.
* Cross-school competitions (such as startup contests, presentation contests, or Model United Nations): train your communication, decision-making, and teamwork skills.

> Tip: When you attend these events, do not focus only on the agenda. Remember to meet new people actively. These relationships may become important resources later.


### 3. Company visits and workshops: connect with industry
If you want to understand workplace culture in advance, company visits and workshops are a great opportunity. Examples include:
* Company visits: step into the company directly, talk with people in the industry, and learn about trends and workplace culture.
* Company workshops: skill-training events hosted by companies, such as presentation skills, design thinking, or data analysis.
* Mentorship programs: some companies or communities offer mentor matching so students can learn from industry veterans.

> Tip: During company visits, be brave enough to ask questions, talk to the speakers, and even stay in touch on LinkedIn. That may lead to future career opportunities.


### 4. Campus ambassadors: the bridge between brands and students
Many companies run campus ambassador programs, where students act as brand representatives, help promote events, and gain access to internal resources. Examples include:
* Google Developer Groups On Campus
* Microsoft Learn Student Ambassadors
* AWS Educate campus ambassador programs

As a campus ambassador, you will have the chance to:

* Directly interact with companies and gain internal information and resources.
* Join company events to improve your professional skills and expand your network.
* Lead campus activities and learn organization, marketing, and leadership skills.

> Tip: When applying for campus ambassador programs, emphasize your passion, relevant experience, and understanding of the brand to improve your chances.

### 5. Cold outreach: learn to take initiative
Cold outreach may sound difficult, but it is actually a very practical skill. Whether you are running a community, planning your career, or even starting a business, you will need it. For example:

* LinkedIn networking: proactively reach out to industry professionals and ask for career advice.
* Recruiting speakers: if you are organizing an event, contact companies or experts directly and invite them to speak.
* Finding collaboration opportunities: whether it is cross-club cooperation or negotiating sponsorships, you need to take the first step.

> Tip: When reaching out, explain your purpose clearly and concisely, for example: "Hello, I am the organizer of XXX club and would like to invite you to speak. Here is the time and place..." That increases the chance of getting a response.


## Conclusion: There are plenty of resources, the real question is whether you are willing to act
These resources are only the tip of the iceberg. The key is whether you are willing to take the first step, participate boldly, learn, and try. Remember, nobody is born a leader; every skill is built through real-world practice. So starting today, try attending an event, joining a club, or reaching out to an industry professional. You will find yourself growing one step at a time! 💪🔥


# Closing thoughts
For me, the journey from "managing" to "leading" was challenging, but also deeply rewarding. I learned not only how to manage a team, but also how to truly lead a community forward.
If you are interested in becoming a community leader, I encourage you to start small, try bravely, and build experience. Most importantly, do not be afraid of failure, because every challenge is an opportunity to grow.
